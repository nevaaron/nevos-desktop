# The Curse of the Country Song

Everybody knows country music, and a lot of people hate it. I am one of those people.

But this isn't actually about country music.

This is about that weird thing we all do where we passionately chase some goals, then quietly abandon others. Not because we stopped caring, but because something invisible got in the way.

So I'm going to try to answer that question via a banjo.

## The Dream I Gave Up On

I've wanted to write a country song for about twenty years.

It started when I was twelve, listening to the second verse of Johnny Cash's *Folsom Prison Blues*. It's the line where he says:

> "I shot a man in Reno just to watch him die."

That lyric felt like a nightmare in three acts. First, the vivid murder. Second, the motive, cruel but weirdly self-aware. Third, the aftertaste, the part where you realise the joke is on you, because a tiny piece of you is cheering for the killer.

Not because you actually want to be that guy, but because Johnny Cash sounds so damn cool.

That was the first time a creative work set my head on fire.

## Astral Yeehaws

I give dumb names to feelings. I don't know why, I just do.

A transcendent moment in art, the kind that makes you feel like you've left the planet, I call an **astral yeehaw**. It's my tribute to Johnny Cash and that gut punch he gave me as a kid.

If you chase that feeling, you eventually start wanting to *make* it. Writing, drawing, documentaries, whatever your medium is.

For me, even though the obsession started with country music, somehow it's been twenty years and I've still never written a country song.

And that ends today.

## Meet Adam (My Half a Plan)

I had half a plan, and his name is Adam.

Adam lives in a one-bedroom cabin in the middle of nowhere, off-grid, totally beautiful. His roommate is Penny the spider. He's got a big heart, a voice of an angel, and the chaotic energy of someone who can be equal parts impulsivity and soul.

One week earlier, Adam made what he called his dumbest decision to date:

> "One of the things that's on my bucket list, and this has been on my bucket list for a long long long time, is to write and record a country song."

I said yes immediately.

We hit the road.

## The Thing That Was Stopping Me

At first, the process felt heavy. Tight. Like we were trying to force the song into existence.

And eventually I realised the problem wasn't a lack of talent, or time, or even motivation.

It was my relationship to failure.

I have a little internal vocabulary for this stuff:

- Astral yeehaw, the transcendent moment.
- The pocket zone, the flow state.
- A bubble, that sudden realisation where everything connects.
- And **hiccups**, mistakes.

While we were stuck, I had a bubble about hiccups.

Mistakes are not the enemy of creativity. They're the path.

I know this when I draw. You make a mark, the mark is wrong, but it's wrong in a way that suggests something new. So you follow it. You turn the mistake into a choice.

But when I picked up the mandolin, I forgot the lesson.

The less open you are to failure, the less open you are to success.

## The Holy Grail of Creativity

My favourite example of a hiccup becoming a masterpiece is in a Dolly Parton song, *Do I Ever Cross Your Mind*.

In the version she did with Chet Atkins, around the 50-second mark, Dolly giggles while she's singing about heartbreak.

She didn't plan it. When she remade the song later, it's not there.

But in that original version, the giggle is a hiccup so perfect it becomes the astral yeehaw.

That's the holy grail of creativity.

The chimes that fall over in *Fantasia*. The unfinished portrait of a soldier. The improvised rant that makes a live crowd lose their minds.

The mistake becomes the thing.

## Why The Song Wouldn't Come Out

If hiccups are the path, why was I avoiding them?

Because I needed it to work.

That desperation turned into over-planning, obsessing over verse structures and pre-choruses like there was going to be a scoreboard at the end. I was trying to follow an equation.

And we all know an equation is not a great way to make something that feels alive.

Worse than that, it is so not country.

Country is messy. Honest. Human.

So we flipped the switch.

## Pressure Off

When the pressure came off, everything changed.

We stopped fighting hiccups and started letting them happen.

And then something interesting happened.

With the pressure so off that I wasn't even filming, I was outside cooking sausages, you could hear a song being written in the background.

The track got written in less than an hour.

Not because the people involved suddenly got smarter. Because they got freer.

## Marrickville, Punk Banjo, and Gang Vocals

We took that mentality into the studio.

Not the cabin anymore, this was city air, big smoke, big smells. The song got weird in the best way.

Banjo not being played properly, then being played properly.

Gang vocals that were more gang than vocal.

Parts that slipped, then stayed slipped, because the slip was the hiccup baby.

The point wasn't to produce a perfect artefact.

The point was to let the artefact be alive.

## So Why Do We Abandon Some Goals?

I started this with a question.

Why do we chase some goals and abandon others?

Unsatisfyingly, there are a million reasons.

Sometimes it's out of our control. Other times it's self-sabotage. I don't think there is a universal answer.

But I did realise something related, and it's arguably more important.

The goals we abandon have the power to do more for us than hitting them ever could.

Failure teaches us in a way that success doesn't.

Knowing what not to do is often the fastest path to knowing what to do.

In weirder words:

- The goals we hit are the astral yeehaws.
- The goals we give up on are the hiccups.

And if writing this song is anything to go off, the hiccups are not the opposite of yeehaws.

They're the key that unlocks them.

Failure can be brutal. It can make you feel shame until you never want to try again.

But it also has the power to make you grow more than anything else in this world.

If you've got demons in your closet, gaps in your resume, and hiccups in your head, you've got backstage access to the rawest truths and the richest stories.

We can't change the fact that highs and lows are inevitable.

But we can change what that means.

And I don't know why we make the mistakes that we do.

But I do know that they make damn good country songs.
