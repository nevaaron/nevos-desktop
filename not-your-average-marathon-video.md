# Not Your Average Marathon Video

Quick hypothetical.

Could you run a marathon right now?

Probably not.

But what if there was a gun to your head?

Suddenly you discover a weird truth: you are capable of more than you think, provided the stakes are high enough.

The problem is, in real life, nobody is holding a gun to your head. So the question becomes:

**Can you create stakes that feel just as real?**

## The Problem With Willpower

I have never seen myself as an athlete.

As a kid I had a soccer trophy that basically said: we came last, but we showed up. That was my brand.

So whenever I hear about someone doing something insane, like David Goggins running 100Ks, my brain does this thing where it files it into the category of:

> "Not for me. Impossible. Different species."

But there is always that tiny slither of possible.

And that slither is dangerous, because it means the only thing really stopping you is the story you keep telling yourself.

## The Dumbest Commitment Device Ever

I needed a way to make a marathon feel necessary.

So I made a plan so stupid it almost worked.

I filmed a video that would absolutely get me cancelled, scheduled it to go live in 24 hours, then handed over my wallet and locked myself out of YouTube.

The only way to stop that video was to get back to my laptop.

The laptop was 42.2 kilometres away.

So now running a marathon was not a fitness goal. It was damage control.

This is what psychologists call a **Ulysses pact**.

It is named after Odysseus, who wanted to hear the sirens' song, but also knew it would hijack his willpower and kill him.

So he did two things:

- He put wax in his sailors' ears so they could not hear the sirens.
- He had them tie him to the mast so even if he begged, he could not jump overboard.

A Ulysses pact is you tying yourself to the mast.

It is a plan you make when you are thinking clearly, designed to protect you from the version of you who will panic, bargain, and quit.

## Burning the Ships

This idea shows up everywhere.

Cortez allegedly burned his ships so his men could not retreat.

Some birds teach their chicks to fly by throwing them out of the nest.

It sounds brutal, but it creates a simple condition:

**There is only forward.**

So I got dropped off 42.2 kilometres away from my computer.

And then I started running.

## Chaos, Wrong Turns, and an Underwater Route

I had decided to do this less than 24 hours earlier.

So of course, within the first kilometre, my route was underwater.

Then I got lost.

Then I ended up in somebody's backyard.

And the entire time, the only thought that kept me moving was this:

> "I can't not do it."

There is an odd comfort in that.

When you remove the option to quit, the mind stops negotiating and starts solving.

## The Moment I Quit

At around 32 kilometres, I hit the wall.

Dizzy, vomiting, genuinely cooked.

I decided to stop.

And then I did the thing I am not proud of.

I called my wife and asked her to cancel the scheduled video.

She said: maybe.

Not because she is cruel, but because she is smart.

She wanted verification.

So I found my friends, told them what was happening, and they did what good friends do.

They did not care about the bit.

They cared about me.

And then Aaron, the guy filming, hit me with the most cliché sports-movie monologue imaginable.

It worked.

## The Second Half Was the Easiest

There is a saying about marathons:

> The first half is the first 20 miles.
> The second half is the final 6.

Same effort, different distances.

So I braced for hell.

But the weirdest thing happened.

That final 10K was the easiest 10K I have ever run.

Light. Smooth. Fast.

I ran with my wife and my daughter at one point.

My legs felt good.

My brain felt quiet.

Somewhere along the way, the insane motivation device faded into the background and it just became, simply, a run.

## What Actually Ends At the Finish Line

A few days later I realised something.

I used to see marathons the way bucket lists sell them: a thing you tick off.

But finishing did not feel like the end of a book.

It felt like the beginning of a different life.

Because if you can do something you genuinely believed was impossible, you start looking around like:

**What else am I wrong about?**

When you cross a finish line, literal or metaphorical, the thing that ends is not the activity.

You will run again.

The thing that ends is your former self.

The person who thought the marathon was impossible no longer exists.

That finish line is their finish line.

And for the person you become, the person fuelled by how possible everything suddenly seems, that line is where they start.
